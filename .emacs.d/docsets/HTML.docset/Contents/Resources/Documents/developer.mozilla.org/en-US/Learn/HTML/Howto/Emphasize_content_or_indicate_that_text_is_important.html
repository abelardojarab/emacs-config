    Emphasize content or indicate that text is important

    by 3 contributors:
    Show Sidebar
    In This Article
    1. Emphasis
    2. Strong importance
    3. Relevance
    4. Related semantics
    5. Learn more

    Learn how to mark up distinctive passages, especially based on importance, relevance, and change in tone of voice.

    Prerequisites: You need to know how to create a basic HTML document.
    Objective: Learn about how to mark passages in your document as special or important for the reader.

    Speech isn't just a string of words: your tone of voice conveys meaning, too. In paper books, punctuation and formatting convey part of this meaning visually, rather than audibly. Either way, the goal is to add implicit information beyond the words themselves.

    Let's take a simple example:

    I'm late.

    I'm late?

    In this example, the first sentence says something (I know I'm late), whereas the second asks a question or expresses surprise, even without changing the words or their order. We use punctuation to get that message across in writing. In speech, we use tone of voice.

    Formatting accomplishes a role similar to punctuation. Here's another example:

    I'm nice, even if sometimes I'm mean.

    This sentence is very neutral (is the person more nice than mean, or more mean than nice?). Let's change that:

    I'm nice, even if sometimes I'm mean.

    I'm nice, even if sometimes I'm mean.

    Did you notice the subtle change? It makes a difference if we're stressing the "nice" part or the "mean" part. In print, we often convey stress by formatting (like italics). We need context to understand the exact meaning, but formatting clearly conveys some meaning, just as tone of voice does.

    This article is not about how to add formatting to your document. That's not HTML's job; you can use CSS to control all visual aspects of your webpage. This article is about how to record the meaning that formatting conveys, and HTML is very much involved with that. Once the meaning is set down (agnostic to presentation), then CSS can present that meaning visually, or screen readers can do so audibly.

    We'll discuss here three different general meanings that HTML can record: emphasis, strong importance, and relevance.

    Emphasis

    The <em> element (<em> for emphasis) carries the same meaning as stressing something in your speech.

    Are we going <em>shopping</em> tonight?
Are we going shopping <em>tonight</em>?

    A graphical browser displays <em> text in italics by default, but you can freely change the visual presentation with CSS. Screen readers read emphasized text in a different tone of voice, just as you do. In short, <em> means emphasis, not italics.

    Strong importance

    The <strong> element indicates serious importance, a sort of super emphasis.

    <p>
  Do <strong>not</strong> touch these chemicals.
  These chemicals are <strong>corrosive!</strong>
</p>

    Again, graphical browsers display <strong> text as bold by default, but <strong> means seriously important, not bold. You can freely change formatting with CSS. As before, screen readers read important text differently, because of the <strong> element rather than because the text is bold.

    Relevance

    Whereas <strong> means "text important in its own context", <mark> means "text related to what the reader's already looking at". The traditional example is search results:

    <p>
  "that" occurs 4 times in the search text:
  This is the cat <mark>that</mark> killed
  the rat <mark>that</mark> ate the
  malt <mark>that</mark> lay in the house
  <mark>that</mark> Jack built.
</p>

    Again, you can assign whatever formatting you like. The default is yellow background:

    Beware when using <mark> to identify search results. If the search returns partial words as matches, you could end up with words broken apart by <mark> tags, like this: I like <mark>the</mark>m  (I like them). That's an accessibility violation, because screen readers don't know to treat broken words as whole words.

    Related semantics

    When you find yourself wanting visual formatting, think about the meaning you're looking to convey. If you're conveying the meaning reflected by <em>, <strong>, or <mark>, by all means use them.

    Some other meanings traditionally associated with italics and bold have their own elements:

    • <cite> for titles of sources
    • <dfn> for the first mentions of keywords
    • <q> for inline quotations
    • <var> for math variables

    But HTML doesn't cover every use case individually, because then using HTML properly would be much more of a pain. The general rule is, if you want visual effects but there's no HTML element corresponding to your intended meaning, you have to use the semantically neutral <span> element with CSS styling. It's very useful to add your own semantics with a class attribute.

    The elements we've discussed so far (except for <span>) all have clearcut associated semantics. The situation with <b>, <i>, and <u> is somewhat more complicated. They came about so people could write bold, italics, or underlined text in an era when CSS was still supported poorly or not at all. HTML5 has retrofitted the elements with somewhat confusing semantic roles.

    Here's the best rule of thumb: it's likely appropriate to use <b>, <i>, or <u> instead of <span>, to convey a meaning traditionally conveyed with bold, italics, or underline, provided there is no more suitable element. However, it always remains critical to keep an accessibility mindset. The concept of italics isn't all that helpful to people using screen readers, or to people using a writing system other than the Latin alphabet.

    • <i> to convey a meaning traditionally conveyed by italic: Foreign words, taxonomic designation, technical terms, a thought...
    • <b> to convey a meaning traditionally conveyed by bold: Key words, product names, lead sentence...
    • <u> to convey a meaning traditionally conveyed by underline: Proper name, misspelling...

    A kind warning about underline: People strongly associate underlining with hyperlinks. Therefore, on the Web it's best to underline only links. Use the <u> element when it's semantically appropriate, but consider using CSS to change the default underline to something more appropriate on the Web. The example below illustrates how it can be done.

    <!-- scientific names -->
<p>
  The Ruby-throated Hummingbird (<i>Archilocus colubris</i>)
  is the most common hummingbird in Eastern North America.
</p>

<!-- foreign words -->
<p>
  The menu was a sea of exotic words like <i lang="uk-latn">vatrushka</i>
  and <i lang="id">nasi goreng</i> and <i lang="fr">soupe à l'oignon</i>.
</p>

<!-- a known misspelling -->
<p>
  Someday I'll learn how to <u class="spelling-error">spel</u> better.
</p>

<!-- Highlight keywords in a set of instructions -->
<ol>
  <li>
    <b>Slice</b> two pieces of bread off the loaf.
  </li>
  <li>
    <b>Insert</b> a tomato slice and a leaf of
    lettuce between the slices of bread.
  </li>
</ol>

    /* Typical alternative styling rules */

b {
  font-weight:  normal;
  font-variant: small-caps;
}

.spelling-error {
  text-decoration: underline red wavy;
}
